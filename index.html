Thank you for your interest in the ECHO Research Program

The goal of this research is to understand how certain chemicals affect the blood and to develop a device to tell if somebody has been exposed to these chemicals.

This research will help us better understand how chemicals affect the body and help the US Department of Defense protect the United States from terrorism.

Take the Survey

About Echo

The ECHO research program is a Department of Defense project to create a device that can tell whether somebody has been using explosives, chemical or biological weapons, or radioactive material.

This device will work by looking for “epigenetic” signals, which are changes in the makeup of the chemicals in the blood stream. These signals in your blood can vary with age, illness, and chemical exposure to a dangerous substance.

We are collecting blood samples from people who:

  • Worked for more than 1 year in a facility with any of the following chemicals: TNT, RDX, PETN, or thiodiglycol, or

  • Did NOT work in a facility with following chemicals: TNT, RDX, PETN, or thiodiglycol.

The Study

What is the study about?

We are asking you to participate in a research study. Participation in this study is voluntary. This site is designed to give you information about this study where we will describe this study and answer any of your questions.

If you choose to participate, you will be given a gift card to compensate you for the time you have spent participating.

Participation

What am I agreeing to if I participate?

If you volunteer to participate, you will be asked to fill out a form about your personal history and give blood. Participation is entirely voluntary, and you can withdraw from the study and leave at any time. There is no penalty if you choose to withdraw.

The survey will help us decide whether you are qualified to participate. People who are qualified will be asked to complete additional questions, including questions about your contact information and about your height, weight, gender, race, etc. It will also include general questions about your health history, medications, and lifestyle. Many of these questions are voluntary. Voluntary questions are marked on the survey and you may choose not to answer them and still participate in the ECHO program. These questions will help us understand how other things such as age or medical history affect the epigenetic signals in your blood. The survey should take less than 30 minutes to complete.

After completing the survey, you will be asked to give blood. We will be collecting four, 9 mL vials of blood (just over a fluid ounce of total blood) and one, 2.5 mL vial of blood from your arm. The process will be similar to the process of giving blood for a medical test. A certified phlebotomist (a medical professional who is specifically trained to draw blood) will draw the blood. The blood collection should take less than 10 minutes, though you may be asked to wait briefly between the survey and blood collection.

The Process

You will be asked to fill out a survey and provide a blood sample as part of your participation. The survey will include questions about your demographics (e.g. age and ethnicity), work history, and some medical history such as medications or chronic illnesses. Filling out this survey should only take 30 minutes.

Data Security

Your answers to the survey will be kept anonymous. While we will do our best to protect your information, if there is a data breach your responses are unlikely to affect your employability, reputation, or stigmatize you in any way..

The Blood Sample

The blood sample will be taken from your arm using a needle, similar to blood draws at your doctor’s office. This blood draw will only take about 15 minutes at most.

Who is qualified to participate?

The requirements for participating in the control group are:

You have no known exposures to TNT, RDX, PETN, or thiodiglycol

You are in general good health at the time of sample collection (i.e. not currently sick)

You are between 18 and 65 years old

You have lived in a town that has a business that makes one of the listed chemicals for more than one year

You are not recreationally using any federally controlled substances

You are not pregnant, suspect you may be pregnant, or trying to become pregnant.

What am I agreeing to if I participate?

If you agree to participate in the ECHO research program, you are agreeing to:

  • Fill out the survey including, if you choose, voluntary questions about your health, medications, and lifestyle.
  • Provide blood samples taken by a certified phlebotomist.
  • Allow researchers to use your blood samples and survey responses to look for epigenetic signals related to exposure to TNT, RDX, PETN, or thiodiglycol. This may include sequencing or decoding parts of your DNA to determine how exposure affects different systems in the body.
  • Epigenetic data from your blood sample will be collected and made available to the Department of Defense and other researchers participating in the ECHO research program
  • EVEN IF YOU AGREE TO PARTICIPATE IN THE BEGINING, YOU CAN WITHDRAW AT ANY TIME DURING THE BLOOD DRAWING PROCESS.

Benefits

The research itself will not benefit you because it cannot be used to guide healthcare or lifestyle decisions and no information specifically about you will be returned to you. However, information from this study may benefit people now or in the future. If the project is successful, all of society will benefit from increased security by the improved identification of terrorists and a better understanding of how the human body works when exposed to certain chemicals.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How will you protect my privacy if I participate?

While we will be collecting personal information, we are taking your privacy seriously . Here are the steps we are taking to protect your privacy:

  • All of the researchers who work with your samples will not have access to your name, address, or any other contact information. You will be identified in their records only by a participant code.
  • The document that matches individuals with their participant code will not be accessible by researchers and will be stored separately from the survey responses and blood samples. We will limit and track who has access to this information.
  • All data will be kept in password protected files and stored on secure computers. Physical records and samples will be kept in locked cabinets or freezers inside of locked offices.
  • If there is a data breach, we will tell you.
  • Please note that we may be required to give your contact information to the Department of Defense’s Human Research Protection Office (HRPO). HRPO is allowed by law to review our records to ensure that we’re following the law and not taking advantage of participants like yourself. This review is in no way an attempt or desire to identify participants.

Can I participate anonymously?

If you do not want us to contact you for any reason after you provide a blood sample, you may participate anonymously. This reduces the risk of loss of privacy. Please note that we may also be required by law to provide your contact information to the Department of Defense Human Research Protection Program. The Department of Defense may want this information to ensure we have complied with US federal laws and regulations.

What risks or discomforts are associated with participation?

If you chose to participate, there are two potential risks:

  1. A breach of privacy: while we will work to protect your data, it is possible that your data or samples may be stolen or accessed by unauthorized parties. The study team is taking several steps to protect your privacy and reduce this risk (see “How will you protect my privacy if I participate?”).
  2. Injury during blood draw: if you consent to a blood draw, there is a chance you may suffer side effects from the draw. These typically include brief pain and bruising at the site of the blood draw. Some people may become dizzy or feel faint. There is also a small risk of infection. A certified phlebotomist will collect all the blood samples to reduce these risks and ensure your safety.

What is the cost of participation?

There is no cost to participate in the ECHO research program. In fact, we will compensate you for the time you spend filling out the survey and giving a blood sample.
